## Summary

OpenAI publicly supports Illinois state bill SB 3444, which shields AI labs from liability for critical harms caused by frontier models. The bill defines frontier models as those trained with over $100 million in computational costs. Under the proposed legislation, AI labs would be required to publish safety reports but would gain legal protection from direct liability claims related to model outputs.

## Bill Overview: SB 3444 — Artificial Intelligence Safety Act

Illinois SB 3444, sponsored by Sen. Cunningham, establishes a liability framework for developers of frontier AI models. The bill provides that a developer of a frontier AI model shall not be held liable for critical harms caused by the model if the developer did not intentionally or recklessly cause the critical harms and the developer publishes a safety and security protocol and transparency report on its website.

Several AI policy experts have noted that SB 3444 — which could set a new standard for the industry — is a more extreme measure than bills OpenAI has supported in the past.

## Key Provisions

### Frontier Model Definition

The bill applies to AI models trained using more than **$100 million in computational costs**. This threshold means the legislation would likely cover America's largest AI labs, including OpenAI, Google, xAI, Anthropic, and Meta.

### Critical Harms Definition

SB 3444 defines "critical harms" as events such as:

- **Mass casualties**: Death or serious injury of 100 or more people
- **Property damage**: At least $1 billion in property damage
- **Weapons development**: A bad actor using AI to develop a chemical, biological, radiological, or nuclear (CBRN) weapon

### Liability Shield Conditions

Developers gain legal protection from liability claims when the following conditions are met:

1. The developer did **not intentionally or recklessly** cause the critical harm
2. The developer has published a **safety and security protocol** on its website
3. The developer has disclosed a **transparency report** publicly

### Covered Scenarios

The bill shields developers from liability when AI models:

- Are misused by bad actors to create CBRN weapons
- Independently commit criminal acts leading to critical harms
- Cause mass casualties or large-scale property damage (provided the above conditions are satisfied)

## OpenAI's Position

OpenAI stated:

> "We support approaches like this because they focus on what matters most: Reducing the risk of serious harm from the most advanced AI systems while still allowing this technology to get into the hands of the people and businesses — small and big — of Illinois."

> "They also help avoid a patchwork of state-by-state rules and move toward clearer, more consistent national standards."

In testimony, OpenAI's **Caitlin Niedermeyer** struck a message consistent with the Trump administration's crackdown on state AI safety laws, claiming it is important to avoid "a patchwork of inconsistent state requirements." Niedermeyer argued that state laws can be effective if they "reinforce a path toward harmonization with federal systems."

## Expert Analysis

Policy experts believe this bill has a slim chance of passing, given Illinois' reputation for aggressively regulating technology. However, the bill could set a precedent for AI liability frameworks at the state level and influence future federal legislation.

Critics have raised concerns that the bill effectively allows companies to evade accountability for catastrophic outcomes as long as they claim lack of intent and publish safety documentation — a low bar given the potential scale of harms covered.
